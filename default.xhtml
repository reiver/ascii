ASCII

by Charles Iliya Krempeaux

ASCII (short for American Standard Code for Information Interchange) is a historically important character-set that people billions of people have used either on its own or embedded in other character-sets such as CP437 (code page 437), Unicode, and others.

ASCII includes symbols for the upper-case (A-Z) and lower-case (a-z) versions of the English-language alphabet, symbols for arabic-numerals (0-9), symbols for many English-language punctuation marks, symbols for some diacritical marks, and control-codes.

ASCII has had a large impact on computing, on computer-technologies, on computer-software, on computer-hardware, on computer-networks, on the Internet, on the Web, and on human-cultures that interacted with any of these. Of course, ASCII was heavily influenced by things that existed during and before its creation — and thus, ASCII's influence on humanity was part of broader phenomena.

As of the year 2025, the ASCII character-set is still in use as part of the Unicode character-set. And, often by itself, for many Internet computer-protocols.

128 Characters

ASCII has 128 characters — numbered 0 to 127.

Although many (probably most) people who have used ASCII have used it as part of character-sets with more-than 128 characters (such as CP437 (code page 437) and Unicode), ASCII itself has only 128 characters.
